<!-- Despite the inequalities and confusions that God placed among the particles of matter at the beginning. The laws which He imposed on nature cause them to eventually become almost all of the same size and of a moderate motion. They thus took on the form of the air-aether.

To consider matter as it might have been before God began to move it, we must imagine it as the hardest and most solid body that could exist. 

And since no part of such a body can be pushed without pushing or pulling all the others at the same time, we must think that the action or force to move and divide itself—once placed in some of its parts—spread and was distributed instantly into all the rest, as evenly as possible.

It is true that this equality could not be perfect.
First, because there is absolutely no void in this new World, it was impossible for all parts of matter to move in straight lines.

But since they were almost equal and could be diverted as easily one as another, they all had to agree together upon some circular movements. And yet, since we suppose that God moved them in different ways at the beginning, we must not think that they all agreed to turn around a single center, but around many different ones, which we can imagine placed in various locations relative to each other.

From this it follows that the parts must naturally have been less agitated, or smaller, or both, in the regions closest to these centers, and more agitated and larger in the regions farther away.
For since all parts have a tendency to continue moving in a straight line, it is certain that the strongest—i.e., the largest among those equally agitated, and the most agitated among those equally large—must have traced the largest circles, as those most similar to straight lines.

The matter found between three or more of these circles may at first have been much less divided and less agitated.
And moreover, because we suppose God put every kind of inequality among the parts at the beginning, we must think that there were then all sorts of sizes, shapes, and tendencies to move or not move in every direction.

But this does not prevent the fact that the parts later became almost all equal, especially those that remained at the same distance from the centers around which they turned. Since none could move without the others, the more agitated had to communicate their motion to the less agitated, and the larger ones had to break or divide themselves so they could pass through the same places as those before them—or rise higher.
Thus they arranged themselves in a short time in such a way that each part found itself more or less distant from the center according to whether it was more or less large and more or less agitated compared with the others.

Because size always hinders speed, the parts farthest from each center must have been those that, being slightly smaller than the inner ones, were nevertheless much more agitated.

As for their shapes: even though we suppose that at the beginning they had every kind of shape—with many angles and sides like pieces flying from a broken stone—certainly after they moved and collided, they gradually broke the tips of their angles and smoothed the edges of their sides until they became almost all round, like grains of sand and pebbles rolling in a river. So now there can be no notable differences between those that are near each other, nor even between those far apart—except that some move slightly faster or are slightly smaller or larger than the rest.

Only a few must be excepted: those which at the beginning were much larger than the others and could not easily divide, or those of very irregular and obstructive shape which preferred to join together rather than break and round themselves. These retained the form of the Third Element and served to compose the planets and comets, as I will explain later.


Furthermore, the matter that came from around the parts of the Second Element—as these parts broke and lost the sharp points of their angles—must necessarily have acquired a much faster movement than theirs, along with an ease of dividing and changing shape at every moment to fit the places where it found itself. This formed the First Element, as I explained earlier. It had to acquire a much faster movement: the reason is evident.

Since it had to pass through very narrow spaces between the parts of the Second Element as they collided head-on, it had much more distance to travel in the same time.

Also note that whatever portion of this First Element exceeds what is needed to fill the small intervals around the round parts of the Second Element must withdraw toward the centers around which these latter are turning, because they occupy all more distant places.

There, this First Element forms perfectly round, liquid, and subtle bodies, which turn unceasingly much faster and in the same direction as the Second Element surrounding them. They increase the agitation of the nearby parts and push all the others outward, drawing motion from the center to the circumference. This action—soon to be explained more precisely—is what we will call Light.

We will also take these round bodies composed purely of the First Element as the Sun and the fixed Stars of the new World described here, and the matter of the Second Element that turns around them as the Heavens.

Imagine that the points S, E, e, A are the centers I refer to, and that all the matter in the space F–G–G–F is a Heaven turning around the Sun marked S; and that all the matter in the space H–G–G–H is another Heaven turning around the Star marked e, and so on.
Thus there are as many different Heavens as Stars—an indefinite number—and the Firmament is nothing other than the surface, without thickness, that separates all these Heavens from each other.

Also think that the parts of the Second Element near F or G are more agitated than those near K or L. Their speed decreases from the outer circumference of each Heaven up to a certain zone (such as sphere K around the Sun and sphere L around the star e). From there, speed increases again little by little toward the centers due to the agitation of the stars found there.

Thus, while the parts near K have time to complete a single circle around the Sun, those nearer T (which I suppose to be ten times closer) do not merely have time to complete ten—but perhaps more than thirty. And those near F or G (two or three thousand times farther) may complete more than sixty.
From this you can understand that the higher planets must move more slowly than the lower or nearer ones—and also more slowly than comets, though these are farther away.

As for the size of each part of the Second Element: from the outer circumference of Heaven F–G–G–F to circle K, we can think they are equal in size—or that the higher ones are slightly smaller, provided their difference in size is not greater than the difference in their speeds.
But from circle K to the Sun, it is the lowest ones that are the smallest, and their difference in size is at least proportionate to their difference in speed; otherwise, the lower ones (being more agitated and therefore stronger) would displace the higher ones.

Finally, note that because of the way the Sun and fixed Stars form, their bodies may be so small relative to the Heavens that even circles K, L, and similar, marking the extent of their agitation, are negligible compared with the Heavens—like points marking their centers, just as new Astronomers consider the whole sphere of Saturn almost as a point compared to the Firmament.

## The Expansion of the Universe (No Big Bang)

Before God started moving the air-aether, it was the hardest and most solid body in the world. 

God then put inequality and confusion among its particles.

This caused those particles to gradually become the current air-aether, being reduced to:
- one size, and
- one moderate motion


A push from one part would cause a pushing or pulling for all the other parts.

This action or force of moving or dividing was done first in some particles of the air-aether. 

It then spread out and distributed itself in all the other particles at the same instant, as equally as it could.

However, this equality of division could not be perfect because the division could not spread in a straight line since there was no void.

The air-aether particles were all just about equal and easily divisible.
- This allowed them to form together into various circular motions.*

God initially moved them in different ways as different centers, instead of moving them from a single center.*

The strongest particles were:
- the largest ones among those of the same agitation
- the most agitated ones of those of the same size

These strongest particles created the largest circles. 
- Their orbits most closely resembled straight lines.

- They had all sorts of sizes, shapes, and dispositions to move or not to move, in all ways and in all directions.
